Copy of bay blues

I’ve never read anybody write about their hometown in the way that I feel about mine, not because I think my relationship with where I’m from is particularly interesting or unique (it’s not) but because it’s boring.

I grew up in the Silicon Valley, the San Francisco Bay Area, mostly in the city of Mountain View California. It has a population of roughly eighty thousand.

A hometown is supposed to be tender, it is supposed to hold you close, or it should bite and scratch you and tell you to change, and, in either case, it is supposed to bring back childhood tears. A hometown is like a mother. It hurts you, you see all of its flaws in all of its detail, and it makes you see yours. Or so I hear. When you write about your hometown, it’s supposed to be a love letter, and I’ve decided that everything I write is a love letter, so I have to figure out my love for it now.

A hometown is like a mother, or so I hear. Once you grow up and leave, you have to sit with everything you feel towards it, and figure yourself out.

I don’t think my hometown is like my mother. My hometown is for yuppies, of which I am one. It is new, pretty, old, ugly, hypocritical, with everything built on top of itself but full of empty lots. It’s the best and worst place in the world. Everybody in the world wants to come to my hometown, which is why I left, and which is why I might come back.

I am from Mountain View in the San Francisco Bay Area. It doesn’t feel like a hometown. I don't think I get the place I’m from.

A hometown is like a mother, or so I read. Once you grow up and leave, you have to sit with everything you feel towards it, and figure yourself out. You love or hate your hometown, but your heart is filled with tenderness towards it. Your hometown is your childhood, the tears and laughter. A hometown is like a mother. When you inspect its flaws, you inspect your own.

Only my hometown is not like that. The bay area is for yuppies. My mother is not a yuppie. No mother is, because if she was we would call her a mom instead. I get my hometown, on paper, I get why people move here (even beyond the jobs). I understand everything that makes the bay area great. I like good schools and fancy health foods and parks for hiking. I’m sure this would be a great place to raise a family. Logistically. But, honestly, when I’m here, all I can see is what it’s not. It’s still never what I expect from it. Why can’t it be a hometown that you write about?

It’s not as warm as you would expect California to be. There’s lots of empty lots next to new apartments. The roads worth walking are warm and dry and the comforting streets aren’t worth walking. I have never had a car so I don’t feel like I ever conquered here.

Honestly, I think a car may be the key to loving the bay area, or maybe any hometown. It’s not my place to write about cars but the only times I felt like my home belonged to me was when I was driving through it. I usually can’t drive, even my parents’ car, because I don’t have insurance. The bay area doesn’t feel warm. It doesn’t feel like home. I feel like I’m being rejected from the place I am from. Or else I would feel different, right? I don’t hate it either. We are just strangers.
